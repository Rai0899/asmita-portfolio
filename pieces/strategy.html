How We Grew Audience by 45% Without Increasing the Team

Category
Content Strategy
Format
Case study
Audience
Editorial leads, content managers, brand teams
Tone
Honest, practical, grounded in real numbers

Nobody handed me a playbook when I took over the editorial team at IEMA. There was no strategy document sitting on my desk, no brief that explained what we were trying to do or why. What I walked into was a team of five writers, a publication pipeline of over 200 pieces, and a slow, creeping sense that we were producing a lot without going anywhere in particular.

That's not a criticism of what came before me. It's just the reality of most content operations at a certain stage. Everyone has their head down, everyone is hitting deadlines, and no one has had the time or the distance to step back and ask whether the deadlines are actually the right ones to be hitting.

I had that time, barely, in the first few weeks. And what I saw when I looked closely was a team running on effort rather than direction. Pieces were going out because pieces were due. The revision process was long and inconsistent, not because the writers weren't capable, but because nobody had ever written down what good actually looked like at this publication. The audience numbers had flattened, not because the content was bad, but because it was scattered. There was a lot of output and not enough focus. So I started there.

1. I wrote down the standard

This sounds obvious. It wasn't. Before I documented anything, quality lived entirely in editors' heads, mine included. A piece would come in and I would know within two paragraphs whether it was close or not, but I couldn't always explain why in terms a writer could act on before the next draft.

So I wrote it out. What makes a strong lede for our audience. When to use data and when it actually slows the reader down. How to structure an explainer differently from a news piece. The difference between a claim and a verified fact, and why that difference matters even in content that isn't journalism.

It took longer than I expected, because writing the standard forced me to actually understand it rather than just feel it. You cannot document something you only half know. Once it existed, three things changed quickly. Writers had something concrete to write toward before submitting, rather than guessing and reacting to feedback afterward. Revision notes became less about personal preference and more about shared principle, because I could point to the documented thing instead of trying to articulate a feeling. And bringing new writers up to speed got noticeably faster, because the knowledge was in a document rather than locked inside someone's head.

Revision cycles dropped 30% within two months. The document alone didn't do all of that, but it did most of it.

2. We slowed down before writing

We introduced a research step between editorial planning and the actual briefing of a piece. Before anything got assigned, someone had to answer three questions. Who is actually going to read this? What do they already know, and what are they trying to figure out? Is there any real evidence — whether search data or reader questions or traffic patterns — that this is something they are looking for right now?

It felt slow at first. Writers wanted to get going and editors wanted to fill the calendar. The research step felt like unnecessary friction.

But what it stopped was something we had all been doing without quite noticing: commissioning from habit. Assigning pieces because a topic felt vaguely relevant, or because something similar had done reasonably well six months ago, or because a client had suggested it. None of those are terrible reasons, but none of them actually start from the reader.

We began using SEMrush and Google Analytics differently, not as performance reports we checked after publishing but as signals we read before briefing. What questions is our audience searching for that we haven't answered yet? Where are people landing and immediately leaving? What's performing above expectations and what does that tell us about what they actually needed? The calendar started looking more specific and more deliberate. And over that year, audience engagement grew by 45%. I won't oversell the cause, because a lot of things contributed, but shifting from commissioning by assumption to commissioning from evidence was the biggest single change we made.

3. We closed the feedback loop

Every Monday we spent twenty minutes together looking at the previous week's numbers as a group. Not to celebrate or to pick apart what went wrong, just to ask what the data was actually telling us. When a piece did unusually well, we tried to understand why. When something didn't land, we asked whether the problem was in the premise or the framing or somewhere earlier in the process.

Over time, something happened that I hadn't planned for. The team developed a genuine instinct for the audience. Writers stopped guessing about what would resonate because they had spent months watching the patterns closely. The data stopped being a report that lived after publishing and started feeding into every decision that came before it.

Output went up 25% over that same period without adding anyone to the team, not because people worked harder but because we had stopped spending time on work that wasn't working.

Conclusion

That's really the whole of it. Write the standard down, research before you brief, close the feedback loop. Nothing revolutionary in any of those three things. But most content operations I've seen aren't doing any of them, and they're wondering why growth has stalled.

The numbers matter and I'm proud of them. But what I think about more is the team at the end of it: writers who know what good looks like and can explain it to someone else, editors who can articulate why something isn't working rather than just sensing it, a calendar that exists for a documented reason rather than out of inertia. That part is harder to measure, and I think it's probably more valuable in the long run.
