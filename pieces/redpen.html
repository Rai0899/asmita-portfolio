Personal Essay

The Red Pen Nobody Sees

Category
Personal Essay
Format
Narrative essay
Audience
General readers, writers, anyone who works with words
Tone
Personal, reflective, unhurried

The first time I sent back a piece with more comments than words, I felt awful about it.

The writer had submitted a 900-word explainer on adaptive learning. She had done the research and she knew her subject well. The piece wasn't bad so much as it wasn't good yet. The structure was upside down, and the most interesting thing she had found was buried in paragraph six like she was embarrassed by it. Three of her key claims traced back to secondary sources when the originals were only a Google Scholar search away.

I marked all of it, explained all of it, and sent it back.

She rewrote it and it came back completely different, tighter and more confident, finally willing to lead with the thing that actually mattered. When it published, it outperformed almost everything we had put out that month. She messaged me afterward and said she hadn't known she was doing it wrong until I showed her what right looked like. I've thought about that line quite a bit since then.

There are two kinds of editing, and I didn't fully understand this until I found myself doing both at the same time.

The first kind is correction. You find what's wrong, you fix it or tell someone else to, and the piece comes out cleaner on the other side. This is editing as quality control, and it is necessary and unglamorous and fundamentally about the document in front of you. A good copy editor does this, and you genuinely need people who can.

The second kind is harder to name. It's less about the piece and more about the person who wrote it, about trying to understand what they were actually reaching for well enough to help them reach it. The goal isn't to make the piece sound like you. It's to make it sound more like them, only better. The best version of that edit is invisible. The writer reads the published piece and thinks that yes, this is what I meant. They don't remember which sentence you restructured or which paragraph you moved around. That's the whole point.

When I built the editorial quality framework at IEMA, the documented standard that eventually cut our revision cycles by 30%, I was trying to make the second kind of editing something that could scale. To take what existed in my head as instinct and turn it into something a writer could actually hold and use. What does a good lede do? When does context genuinely serve the reader and when does it just delay them getting to something they need? What separates a claim from a verified fact, and why should that distinction matter even in content that isn't journalism?

Writing it out forced me to understand my own standards more precisely than I ever had before. You cannot document something you only half know. The act of writing the framework taught me as much as it ever taught anyone who read it afterward.

I grew up in Kolkata in a house where books were simply part of the furniture, quite literally. Two full shelves in the living room were the first thing you saw when you walked in. My mother read Bengali novels. My father collected English paperbacks with a patience I deeply admired and did not inherit. I never grew up thinking I would work with words professionally. I grew up thinking that's just what words were for, a way of making sense of things.

The career came sideways, the way most things do. An MA in English at Symbiosis taught me to read slowly enough to understand why a sentence worked or didn't, to feel the difference between writing that moved and writing that just sat there on the page doing nothing. Then the actual work taught me everything the degree hadn't: that a deadline is a form of discipline, that an audience is a real person with a finite amount of time and attention, that the comma left in the wrong place is a small act of carelessness toward whoever is reading.

What I hadn't expected was how much of the job would turn out to be about other people's writing.

The National Postal Museum project was unlike anything else I had worked on before, though I didn't fully understand why until I was a few weeks into it. We were producing 25 pages of content for a national heritage institution, educational material and contextual essays and interpretive text about the history of postal communication in India. It was managed carefully and delivered on time and signed off without issues. By every normal measure of a content project, it went well.

But I kept finding myself thinking about who would actually read it, not the stakeholders or the approval committee, but the person who would stand in a gallery years from now and read a panel of text that I had written. Someone we would never meet, for whom a small detail about a colonial-era mail route or a particular postage stamp might land in a way we couldn't predict and couldn't control.

Writing for that person changes how you approach it. It makes you more careful about the claim you cannot fully verify, the detail that is interesting but not quite accurate, the shortcut that saves you a sentence but costs the reader something they deserved. A lot of institutional content gets written as if nobody will really read it, as if the purpose is completion rather than communication. I didn't want to write it that way.

The writer who rewrote her explainer now sends back drafts with long, detailed comments. I know because I've watched her do it. She marks the buried lede. She asks for the primary source. She explains why something isn't working rather than just noting that it isn't. I recognise where it came from, not specifically from me but from the process, from having someone take her work seriously enough to push back on it, from seeing what her piece became once she did the harder version of the revision.

That's the thing about editing that took me a while to properly understand. You're not only working on the piece in front of you. You're working on the person who wrote it, and through them on every piece they'll write after this one. The red pen nobody sees is the one that stays with someone long after the document is closed.
